Landmarks

Knoxville, Tennessee, is a city steeped in history and adorned with a variety of landmarks that showcase its rich heritage. One of the most iconic landmarks is the Sunsphere, a towering structure built for the 1982 World's Fair. Standing at 266 feet tall, the Sunsphere offers panoramic views of the city and the nearby Smoky Mountains. It has become a symbol of Knoxville and a popular spot for both locals and tourists to enjoy breathtaking vistas and learn about the city's World's Fair legacy. Another prominent landmark in Knoxville is the historic Market Square. This vibrant gathering place dates back to the 1850s and has since evolved into a hub of culture, entertainment, and community events. Surrounded by charming shops, restaurants, and live music venues, Market Square hosts a range of festivals and activities throughout the year, bringing residents and visitors together to celebrate Knoxville's arts, culinary delights, and sense of community. Its historical significance and lively atmosphere make it a must-visit destination for anyone exploring the heart of Knoxville's urban city.

History

Knoxville has a captivating history that dates back to its founding in 1786. The city was established by James White, who named it in honor of Secretary of War Henry Knox. Initially serving as the capital of the Southwest Territory, Knoxville played a significant role in the early days of Tennessee's statehood. It became the state capital in 1796 when Tennessee was admitted to the Union, but the capital was later moved to Nashville in 1812. During the Civil War, Knoxville found itself at the heart of the conflict as both Confederate and Union forces sought control of the strategic city. The city changed hands several times during the war, with pivotal battles taking place in and around Knoxville. Notably, the 1863 Battle of Fort Sanders played a crucial role in the Union's efforts to secure control of East Tennessee. Today, the city preserves its historical heritage through various landmarks and museums, offering visitors a chance to delve into Knoxville's role in shaping Tennessee's early history and its resilience during times of war.
